# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Muslims in the USA who pray in congregation, and the parents among them teaching children how to pray. They arrive on an iPhone or a laptop, usually from a shared link or an App Store search, wanting to know in seconds what the app does and whether it can be trusted. A secondary audience is masjid committee members and imams deciding whether to bring their masjid's times onto the platform.

## Product Purpose

The site is the public face of Salat, a free iPhone app that shows the real jama'ah (iqamah) times set by each masjid's own committee, compares every masjid nearby, and gently teaches how each prayer is offered. The site exists to explain the app, earn trust before the App Store listing is live, and invite masjids to contribute their times. Success is a visitor who understands within one screen that these are real committee-set times, not calculated estimates, and who either waits for the App Store release or submits their masjid.

## Positioning

The only prayer app that shows the times your masjid actually follows, not what an algorithm calculated.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary CTA: See the app (scroll into the feature tour); becomes "Download on the App Store" at launch.
- Secondary CTA: For masjids and imams, "How to add your masjid" (support page).
- The line a visitor remembers: "So no one misses the jama'ah."
- Belief ladder: (1) these are real committee-set times, not estimates; (2) it covers the masjids near me and gets me there; (3) it is genuinely free, private, and ad-free; (4) it is built by the community as sadaqah jariyah, so I can trust its motives.
- Proof on hand: real app screenshots and a real screen capture (assets/), the privacy policy's concrete no-tracking commitments, and the in-app community correction loop.

## Brand Personality

Calm, reverent, communal. The voice is the community speaking, never an individual; no personal names appear anywhere. Warm and plain-spoken English sits beside Arabic devotional phrases (bismillah, hayya 'ala as-salah, the closing dua) that are treated with typographic respect, never as decoration. The feeling to evoke: the quiet of a masjid before iqamah - unhurried, trustworthy, beautiful.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS landing pages: gradient-purple heroes, three equal feature cards, hype verbs ("supercharge your worship").
- Ad-cluttered prayer apps and their websites; anything that feels monetized.
- Orientalist kitsch: mosque-silhouette clip art, excessive gold ornament, "Arabian nights" fonts for English text.
- Em dashes in copy. The site's prose voice avoids them entirely.

## Design Principles

- The times are the product: every screen leads back to real iqamah times and the app screenshots that show them.
- Practice the privacy we preach: fully self-contained pages, no third-party requests, no analytics.
- Bilingual reverence: Arabic is a first-class typographic voice, set correctly (RTL, proper fonts), used for devotional phrases and section markers.
- Calm over spectacle: motion follows the day (dawn to night), eases out gently, and never begs for attention.
- Community voice: "we" is the community; credit belongs to no one and the reward to Allah.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Respect prefers-reduced-motion with full alternatives (the site is motion-rich). WCAG AA contrast in both light and dark schemes. Semantic landmarks, skip link, keyboard-reachable disclosure FAQs, correct lang/dir on all Arabic text. The audience spans ages and tech comfort; copy stays simple enough for a child or a new Muslim.
